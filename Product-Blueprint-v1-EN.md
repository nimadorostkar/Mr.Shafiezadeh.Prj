# Product Blueprint v.1

> An English translation of the project blueprint for a long-term personal knowledge and content infrastructure.

## 00. Project Philosophy & Vision

### Introduction

The goal of this project is to build a long-term infrastructure for managing, maintaining, retrieving, and developing my content, intellectual, and professional assets — an infrastructure that, over time and without dependence on any single platform, technology, or passing trend, can become the main home of my digital presence.

Over the past years, a significant portion of my activities, projects, writings, images, documents, experiences, and interests have been published or stored across various platforms and services. Each of these platforms holds a part of these assets, but none of them provides a complete and unified picture of what has been created.

This fragmentation makes it increasingly difficult over time to retrieve knowledge, develop content, reference past projects, and even tell the story of a professional identity.

For this reason, this project was formed with the vision of bringing all these assets together in a coherent, extensible structure that is independent of any particular platform.

### Project Philosophy

This project is built on the belief that content, knowledge, and experience are long-term assets — not temporary outputs for social networks.

Every note, project, image, article, talk, book, experience, or idea is part of a larger network of knowledge and experience whose value is not limited to its initial publication, but is found in its retrievability, its connection to other content, and its reuse in the future.

In this view, the website is only one of the system's outputs — not the system itself.

The core system is an infrastructure for managing and organizing knowledge, content, and professional identity, and the website will be one of the most important ways to display and interact with it.

### Vision

The vision of this project is to create a platform that, in the long run, becomes the main reference for all of my content, intellectual, and professional assets.

This platform must be able to:

- Be the main home of my digital presence.
- Be a trustworthy reference for the information and content I publish.
- Preserve the connection between projects, writings, people, topics, and experiences.
- Allow gradual growth and development without the need for fundamental redesigns.
- Preserve data independence and full ownership of content.
- Make content creation, management, archiving, and retrieval simpler and more sustainable.

In this model, platforms such as LinkedIn, X, Telegram, Behance, and Pinterest play the role of channels for publishing, discovering, and distributing content — but the main reference and center of gravity of this ecosystem will be this platform.

### Mission

The mission of this project is to design and develop a platform that, over the coming years, can manage all content and intellectual assets within a coherent, extensible, and reliable system — such that every new piece of content, instead of merely being published, becomes part of a growing knowledge network.

### Definition of Success

This project will be successful when:

- Every new piece of content has a defined place within the overall structure of the system.
- Finding and reusing content is simple and fast.
- Connections between topics, projects, and experiences form naturally.
- Dependence on third-party platforms is minimized.
- The system can develop over the coming years without fundamental changes.
- Everyday use of it, for me, is simpler than using several scattered tools and platforms.
- This platform becomes the main reference for introducing, maintaining, and developing my professional identity and intellectual assets.

## 01. Problem Definition

### Current Situation

Currently, my digital presence and content assets are scattered across various platforms and services.

For example:

- LinkedIn
- X (Twitter)
- Telegram
- Behance
- Pinterest
- Documents in Google Drive
- Personal files
- Images
- Projects
- Notes
- Published books and writings

Each of these platforms displays a part of my activities, but none of them provides a complete, up-to-date, and reliable picture of my professional identity, intellectual path, and content assets.

### The Core Problem

The core problem is the lack of a single reference for managing, maintaining, and developing content and professional assets.

Currently:

- Content is scattered across various points.
- Connections between projects, writings, and experiences are not well preserved.
- Retrieving old information is time-consuming.
- Republishing or reusing content is difficult.
- Professional identity is fragmented across multiple platforms.
- There is heavy dependence on third-party services.

### Root of the Problem

Over the past years, content creation has always taken place on various platforms.

Each platform has its own rules, limitations, formats, and structure.

As a result:

- Data is stored in multiple locations.
- The information structure is not unified.
- Long-term content management has become difficult.
- Actual ownership of information is, to some extent, dependent on various services.

### The Problem This Project Solves

Its goal is to create a central infrastructure that can:

- Be the main reference for all content assets.
- Preserve the connections between content.
- Make information retrieval easier.
- Facilitate personal knowledge management.
- Make content creation and publishing more structured.
- Reduce dependence on various platforms.
- Enable gradual growth of the system.

### What This Project Is Not

To prevent misunderstanding, it should be made clear that this project is not intended to be:

- Merely a personal introduction website.
- Merely a portfolio.
- Merely a blog.
- Merely a replacement for social networks.
- Merely a CMS for publishing articles.

This project is a combination of a personal knowledge management system, a content archive, a digital-presence hub, and a personal-brand development infrastructure.

### Desired Output

In the end, the expectation is to form an infrastructure that:

- Is the main home of my digital presence.
- Is a credible reference for all content and projects.
- Has the capacity to develop in the coming years.
- Makes publishing content on other platforms easier.
- Protects content assets as long-term capital.
- Enables the development of new capabilities without fundamental changes to the architecture.

## 02. Project Scope

### Purpose of This Chapter

The purpose of this section is to define the boundaries of the project — exactly what is to be built, what will be developed in the future, and what is deliberately outside the scope of this project.

This document helps with decision-making, prioritization, and preventing uncontrolled growth of the project.

### What This Project Includes

In this project, the main focus is on designing and developing a content and knowledge infrastructure that can:

- Be the main reference of my digital presence.
- Manage content and professional assets.
- Enable categorizing, archiving, and retrieving content.
- Preserve connections between different types of content.
- Have long-term development capacity.
- Serve as a foundation for developing future capabilities.

### What Is Out of Scope in the First Version (MVP)

In the first phase, the following are not among the project's goals:

- Building a social network
- Building a forum
- An advanced comment system
- A paid membership system
- Complex admin panels
- A mobile app
- AI-based capabilities
- A Recommendation Engine
- Semantic Search
- An advanced content-publishing automation system
- A multi-user system

Some of these capabilities may be added to the project in the future, but they are not among the goals of the initial version.

### Project Priority

The priority of this project is to create a stable infrastructure — not to implement the greatest number of features.

When several solutions exist, the option chosen is the one that:

- Is simpler.
- Is easier to maintain.
- Has greater development capacity.
- Creates less dependency.
- Produces greater long-term value.

### Definition of Success in Phase One

At the end of the first version, it is not expected that all desired ideas and capabilities will have been implemented.

Success in this phase is achieved when:

- The information architecture has been formed correctly.
- The content model is defined and extensible.
- The path for developing later versions is clear.
- It is possible to enter the first real content and projects into the system.
- The system is ready for everyday use.

### Decision-Making Principle

Throughout the project, every new capability or idea must, before entering the project, answer these questions:

- Does this capability solve a defined problem?
- Is it essential in the first version?
- Does it create long-term value?
- Does it make the user experience or project maintenance more complex?
- Can it be deferred to later versions?

If the answers to these questions are not clear, that capability will not enter the MVP.

## 03. Product Goals

### Introduction

The goal of this project is to create a stable infrastructure that, over the coming years, can become the center for managing knowledge, content assets, and my professional identity.

To reach this vision, the project's goals are defined at three levels:

- Business Goals
- User Goals
- System Goals

### 1. Business Goals

This project must be able to:

**1.1 Create a single reference** — Become the main reference for introducing my activities, projects, writings, and professional identity.

**1.2 Reduce dependence on third-party platforms** — Reduce dependence on social networks and various services, and keep ownership of content and data in my own hands.

**1.3 Increase the value of content assets** — Instead of producing scattered content, enable the creation of lasting, reusable assets.

**1.4 Strengthen the personal brand** — Present a more complete, coherent, and trustworthy picture of my professional path, interests, projects, and thinking.

**1.5 Build infrastructure for future development** — Serve as a foundation for future projects, products, services, and ideas.

### 2. User Goals

Users must be able to:

**2.1 Quick understanding** — In a short time, understand who I am, what topics I work on, and what value proposition I offer.

**2.2 Easy access** — Easily find projects, writings, books, interests, experiences, and other content.

**2.3 Understanding content relationships** — Be able to see and follow the connections between projects, writings, and various topics.

**2.4 Content discovery** — While reading one piece of content, be naturally guided toward related content.

**2.5 Simple interaction** — If they wish, be able to easily get in touch with me or start a collaboration.

### 3. System Goals

The system must:

**3.1 Be extensible** — Allow new content types and new capabilities to be added without the need for fundamental redesign.

**3.2 Be independent** — Have minimal dependence on specific tools, services, and technologies.

**3.3 Be maintainable** — Be designed so that maintenance, updating, and development are simple.

**3.4 Be structured** — Have all content with a defined model, metadata, categorization, and definable relationships.

**3.5 Be searchable** — Information retrieval must be fast, accurate, and simple.

**3.6 Preserve data ownership** — All information, content, and metadata must remain in my own hands, with the ability to export them at any time.

**3.7 Be documentable** — All important product, design, and development decisions must be documentable and traceable.

### Definition of Success

This product is successful when:

- Content creation is simpler than it is today.
- Information retrieval is faster than it is today.
- All content assets are placed within a coherent structure.
- Every new piece of content can connect to other related content.
- Future capabilities can be developed without introducing fundamental complexity.
- Over time, the system becomes more coherent and valuable, rather than growing disorderly.

## 04. Product Principles

### Introduction

These principles are the foundation and criteria for all product, design, and development decisions.

Every new decision, every capability, every change in the user experience, and every technical choice must be aligned with these principles.

In the event of a conflict between several options, these principles will be the reference for decision-making.

### 1. Content is more important than technology.

Technology changes over time, but content and knowledge must be lasting. No technical decision should cause dependency or the loss of content ownership.

### 2. Data must always belong to the project owner.

All content, metadata, files, and relationships must be exportable, transferable, and independent of specific tools. No vital part of the project should be locked into a particular service or platform.

### 3. The product must start simple.

The first version is not meant to be complete. Every new capability must solve a defined problem. Simplicity takes priority over completeness.

### 4. Extensibility is more important than completeness.

The architecture must be designed so that new content types and capabilities can be added in the future without fundamental rewriting.

### 5. Information architecture is more important than appearance.

Visual beauty matters, but if the information structure is not designed correctly, the project will lose its value in the long run.

### 6. Content creation must have minimal friction.

Adding, editing, and maintaining content should not become a complex or time-consuming process. The system should make content creation easier, not harder.

### 7. Every piece of content is part of a network.

No content should be defined as an island. Projects, writings, images, books, people, topics, and other assets must be able to connect to one another.

### 8. Every technical decision must support a product need.

No tool, framework, or technology will be chosen merely because it is popular or new. Technical choices must serve the problem, not the other way around.

### 9. Documentation is part of the product.

Important architectural, product, and development decisions must be recorded. Project knowledge should not remain only in people's minds.

### 10. The system must be resilient to change.

A change in technology, a change in team members, or a change in future needs should not cause the loss of information or require a complete rebuild of the project.

### 11. Quality takes priority over speed.

The goal is not to quickly build a website. The goal is to create an infrastructure that can keep growing for years.

### 12. This project is a long-term asset.

All decisions must be made under the assumption that this system is meant to be used for at least several years. Decisions made merely to meet a short-term need will not be prioritized.

## 07. Domain Model

### Introduction

Before being a collection of pages or capabilities, this project is a collection of entities and the relationships between them.

The purpose of this section is to define the main components of this world and the relationships between them, so that in later stages the information architecture, content model, and database design can be built on top of it.

### Core Entities

At first glance, this system will include the following entities.

**Person** — Various people who are in some way connected to activities, projects, or content.

Examples: myself, collaborators, clients, friends, authors, speakers.

**Article** — All published or archived writings.

Each article can be related to: a project, a topic, a book, a person, an event.

**Note** — Shorter notes that are not necessarily articles but are considered part of the knowledge network.

**Topic** — One of the most important entities of the system. All content must be able to connect to one or more topics.

Examples: personal branding, content management, information architecture, digital marketing, artificial intelligence.

**Tag** — Tags are used for lighter and more flexible categorization. Tags are not a replacement for Topics.

**Collection** — A group of content placed together for a specific purpose.

For example: best articles, selected projects, recommended books.

**Media** — All images, videos, files, and multimedia assets.

**Book** — Books I have read, written, or recommended.

**Film** — Films and documentaries I have introduced or reviewed.

**Event** — Talks, workshops, interviews, meetings, and other events.

**Organization** — Companies, brands, universities, and organizations connected to my projects or background.

**Skill** — Skills that play a role in my projects or professional path.

### Relationships Between Entities

In this system, almost no entity is independent.

Examples of relationships:

- A project is related to several topics.
- An article is related to several projects.
- A person is present in several projects.
- A book is related to several notes.
- An event is related to several articles.
- A topic can connect hundreds of different pieces of content.

### Design Principle

In this system, content should not be stored as islands.

The goal is to build a knowledge network in which each entity can form a meaningful connection with other entities.

In the future, these relationships can serve as the foundation for capabilities such as:

- Related-content suggestions
- Advanced search
- Displaying connections between projects
- Building a Graph View
- Knowledge analysis
- AI-based capabilities

without requiring a fundamental change in the system's architecture.

## 08. Information Architecture

### Introduction

Information architecture is the map that determines how information, content, and the various parts of the system are organized, categorized, and connected to one another.

The purpose of this section is not to design menus or pages, but to design the mental structure of the system.

A good information architecture must be able to:

- Make finding content simple.
- Preserve the connections between different sections.
- Enable future development.
- Prevent the creation of duplicate structures.

### First-Level Structure of the System

In the initial version, the system consists of the following sections:

- Home
- About Me
- Writings
- Collaboration
- Contact

This structure may change during the development of the project, but it must always remain simple, understandable, and extensible.

### Information Architecture Principles

**1. Content-centric** — The site structure must be formed based on content, not based on pages.

**2. Every piece of content has a permanent address** — Every entity must have an independent, stable, and referenceable URL.

**3. Every piece of content has only one main location** — No content should be independently duplicated in multiple locations. Displaying a piece of content in different sections must be done through relationships and references, not duplication.

**4. Multiple entry paths** — Users may enter the system from various paths:

- Google
- Social networks
- Direct links
- Internal search
- Related pages

Therefore, no page should depend on a particular navigation path.

**5. Relationships are more important than hierarchy** — The site structure should not be merely a tree. The user must also be able to move through the connections between content.

For example: an article → a project → a person → an event → a book.

**6. Search is part of the architecture** — Search is not a side feature. Search will be one of the main paths for discovering information.

**7. Minimal navigation** — The main menu should remain as simple as possible. The complexity of the system should not be exposed in the navigation.

### Types of User Movement Paths

The user can move between different sections through:

- Navigation
- Search
- Related pages
- Topic
- Tag
- Collection
- Internal links
- Search engines

### Relational Structure

In this project, two types of structure exist simultaneously.

**Hierarchical structure** — Example: Home → Projects → a project.

**Network structure** — Example: a project → topic → three articles → two events → a book → four other projects.

The ultimate goal is to create a balance between these two models.

### Architectural Principle

The user should not feel they are moving through a collection of separate pages.

They should feel they are discovering different parts of a unified system — a system in which all content, projects, people, topics, and experiences are connected to one another in a meaningful way.

## 10. Content Model

### Introduction

After the content types are defined, the structure of each content type must also be defined.

The purpose of this section is to determine the standard fields, data types, whether they are required or optional, and the role of each field in the system.

This model will be the basis for the design of:

- CMS
- Database
- API
- User interface
- Search engine
- SEO

### Standard Content Structure

All content types, regardless of their nature, follow a shared structure as much as possible.

This makes it so that:

- Development is simpler.
- Search is unified.
- The API remains standard.
- Adding new content types is easier.

| Featured image | Image | ✅ | Project cover |

| Status | Enum | ✅ | Draft / Published / Archived |

| End date | Date | ❌ | End time |
| Topics | Relation | ✅ | Topic |
| Tags | Relation | ❌ | Tags |

| Skills | Relation | ❌ | Skill |
| Related projects | Relation | ❌ | Project |

| Attached files | Media[] | ❌ | Files |
| SEO | Object | ❌ | Search engine metadata |

| Field | Data Type | Required |
|---|---|---|
| Title | Text | ✅ |
| Slug | Text | ✅ |
| Summary | Text | ✅ |
| Body | Markdown / Rich Text | ✅ |
| Featured image | Image | ❌ |

| Tags | Relation | ❌ |

| Related projects | Relation | ❌ |
| Related books | Relation | ❌ |

### Shared Fields

Regardless of content type, all entities must support these fields as much as possible:

- Unique identifier
- Title
- Slug
- Status
- Creation date
- Last edit date
- Author
- Summary
- Featured image
- Metadata
- Relationships with other entities

### Design Principle

As much as possible, the creation of dedicated and duplicate fields should be avoided.

Every new capability must first be examined to see whether it can be implemented using the current data model.

If adding a new field is necessary, the reason for its existence must be documented.

### Content Model Rules

- No content is created without a unique identifier.
- No content is published without a path (Slug).
- Relationships between entities are done through references, not duplication.
- The data structure must be independent of the CMS.
- The data model must be independent of the implementation technology.
- Changing the CMS should not cause a change in the content model.

## 11. Metadata Strategy

### Introduction

In this project, metadata is part of the knowledge infrastructure and plays an important role in categorization, search, connections between content, search engine optimization, processing by AI agents, and future development of the system.

The purpose of this section is to define a unified standard for all structural information stored alongside each piece of content.

### Metadata Objectives

The use of metadata must be able to:

- Make content search more accurate.
- Strengthen the connections between content.
- Make information archiving simpler.
- Enable content filtering.
- Improve display quality in search engines.
- Make information processing by AI systems easier.
- Enable the development of future capabilities.

### Types of Metadata

The system's metadata falls into several main groups.

**1. Identity information** — Information that defines the entity.

Examples: unique identifier, title, slug, content type, publication status.

**2. Temporal information** — Examples: creation date, publication date, last edit date, archive date.

**3. Ownership information** — Examples: author, editor, source, ownership status.

**4. Topical information** — Examples: Topic, Tag, Category, Collection.

**5. Relational information** — Examples: related projects, related articles, related people, related organizations, related books, related events.

**6. Media information** — Examples: featured image, gallery, attached files, video, audio file.

**7. Technical information** — Examples: Canonical URL, Open Graph, Robots, Language, Schema.org, Reading Time.

### Metadata Design Principles

**Principle One** — Metadata must be independent of the user interface.

**Principle Two** — All content types should use shared metadata structure as much as possible.

**Principle Three** — Every piece of metadata must have a defined purpose. The existence of every field must be defensible.

**Principle Four** — Duplicate information should not be stored. When possible, information should be extracted or referenced from other entities.

**Principle Five** — Metadata must be equally usable for both machines and humans.

### Metadata and the Future of the Project

In the future, many of the system's capabilities will be built on this same metadata.

Examples:

- Advanced search
- Related-content suggestions
- Timeline
- Graph View
- Personal dashboard
- Content-production analysis
- AI Assistant
- Knowledge Graph

Therefore, the quality of the metadata design will have a direct impact on the project's development capacity.

### Design Principle

Metadata should not be designed merely for today.

Every field must be created under the assumption that it may in the future be used by humans, search engines, other services, or AI agents.

## 12. Taxonomy

### Introduction

In this project, taxonomy is not merely a tool for categorizing content.

Its goal is to design a shared language for the entire system — a language that determines how content is named, categorized, connected to one another, and retrieved in the future.

A good taxonomy makes it so that:

- Content creation is simpler.
- Search is more accurate.
- Connections between content form more naturally.
- The system can grow without creating chaos.

### Purpose of Taxonomy

The taxonomy must be able to:

- Organize the structure of knowledge.
- Prevent the creation of duplicate categories.
- Create a shared language among content.
- Make future development of the system easier.
- Serve as a foundation for future intelligent capabilities.

### Main Components of the Taxonomy

In this project, four main concepts exist.

**1. Topic** — Topic is the core of knowledge categorization. Every piece of content must be connected to one or more Topics.

Examples: personal branding, knowledge management, product design, information architecture, content management, artificial intelligence.

Characteristics: stable, limited, extensible, time-independent.

**2. Category** — Category determines the type or family of content.

Examples: article, project, note, book, event.

Category indicates "what this content is," not "what it is about."

**3. Tag** — Tag is used for lighter connections.

Examples: MVP, Remote, Next.js, GitHub.

Characteristics: flexible, removable, addable, content-dependent.

A Tag should not take the place of a Topic.

**4. Collection** — Collection is a group of content placed together for a specific purpose.

Examples: best writings, selected projects, writings from the year 1405, recommended books.

Collection is used more for the user experience than for knowledge categorization.

### Naming Rules

To preserve the quality of the taxonomy, the following rules are observed.

**Principle One** — Each concept has only one official name.

**Principle Two** — The creation of synonyms is prevented.

For example: ❌ AI, ❌ Artificial Intelligence, ✅ Artificial Intelligence (or vice versa). One is chosen as the main name.

**Principle Three** — Topics are as stable as possible. Short-term trends should not create independent Topics.

**Principle Four** — Tags can be temporary. If unused, they are removed or merged.

**Principle Five** — Categories rarely change.

### Growth of the Taxonomy

Over time, the taxonomy will develop.

But every new concept must be examined before being added:

- Is it truly a new concept?
- Does it not overlap with one of the current Topics?
- Does it have long-term value?
- Will it also be usable in the future?

### Connection to Other Parts of the System

The taxonomy has a direct impact on the following areas:

- Navigation
- Search
- Related Content
- URL Structure
- SEO
- CMS
- API
- AI
- Analytics

### Design Principle

The goal of the taxonomy is not merely to categorize content.

Its goal is to design a shared language for the entire knowledge ecosystem — such that humans, search engines, developers, and AI agents can all understand the information structure in the same way.
